Illustrated Glossary

Instead of memorising word lists, I want to collect small scenes: a snapshot, a few words, and a story about where I met them. Each image comes from something I actually did or saw – like today’s bouldering session – so the vocabulary is tied to a real feeling, not just a dictionary entry.

Right now, this page is just a starting point. Over time, I’d like to add more scenes from different parts of my life:

  • At the climbing gym (routes, holds, techniques, warm‑ups)
  • In the kitchen (ingredients, tools, sounds, small accidents)
  • On the street (signs, small objects, things people do without thinking)
  • At work or in cafés (screens, notebooks, tiny routines)

The goal isn’t to cover “all the words”, but to build a personal map of language around the things I care about. Every image is an excuse to notice more details, write down a few phrases, and come back later to see how my understanding has changed.

For now, this page will simply collect these snapshots, one by one – a quiet glossary that grows alongside my days.

The Boat, Paddle, and Body Link to heading

Understanding the equipment and basic posture is your foundation.

Paddler: That’s you!

Kayak: The boat you are paddling. There are different types, but the general anatomy remains the same.

Blade: The broad part of the paddle that goes into the water to move you.

Shaft: The handle connecting the blades. This is what you grip.

Power Face: The concave or hollow side of the blade that pushes against the water. This must face back toward you (rearward) to generate forward motion.

Torso Rotation: Using the muscles of your upper body and core, not just your arms, to power the stroke.

Paddler’s Box: Keeping your arms slightly bent and your chest centered, forming a stable “box” structure with your paddle that moves in unison with your upper body.

Basic Propulsion and Maneuvering Link to heading

How do you make the kayak move and go where you want?

Forward Stroke: The foundational movement. You plant one blade (power face rearward) in the water near your toes and pull backward, using torso rotation to propel the kayak.

Backward (Reverse) Stroke: Used to stop or back up. You plant the non-power face of the blade in the water behind you and push forward, away from your body.

Sweep Stroke (Turn): A sweeping movement (like drawing a wide arc or “C”) used specifically for turning the kayak.

  • Forward Sweep: Paddling a wide arc away from the kayak on one side to turn the boat in the opposite direction.

  • Reverse Sweep: Pushing water backward in a wide arc to turn the boat sharply (e.g., to turn into an eddy).

Draw Stroke (Move Sideways): Inserting the blade vertically in the water at your hip and pulling it directly toward the side of the kayak to move the entire boat sideways (like moving next to a dock).

Stability and Safety Concepts Link to heading

Keeping your balance is a primary goal in boating, similar to staying on the bouldering wall.

Center of Gravity: Just like in bouldering, keeping your weight low and centered over the kayak’s centerline is critical for balance and stability.

Loose Hips: In bouldering, you often keep your hips tight to the wall for balance. In kayaking, you want “loose hips.” This means allowing your body above the waist to remain independent from the boat’s motion. If the kayak tilts from a wave or rock, you can adjust your upper body balance without forcing the whole boat over.

J-Lean / Edging: Gently tilting the kayak to one side using your hips and core tension to improve performance and turning (engaging the “edge”). The J-Lean specifically raises one gunwale while keeping your body upright.

Capsize: To tip the kayak over completely so that the paddler is upside down or has fallen out. Learning a self-rescue or T-rescue is essential safety practice.

Wet Exit: The safety technique of intentionally exiting a capsized kayak underwater. It involves finding the cockpit coaming, releasing the spray skirt (if wearing one), and pushing yourself out and upward to the surface.
