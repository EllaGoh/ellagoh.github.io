Notes on learning slowly

Most advice about learning sounds like a race: move fast, consume more, never fall behind. But in real life, the things that stay with me rarely come from rushing. They come from the days when I sit with one idea a little longer than necessary.

Learning slowly doesn’t mean avoiding hard work. It means accepting that depth has a different tempo than urgency. Reading the same paragraph twice can feel inefficient, but sometimes that’s how understanding actually arrives — not as a flash, but as a quiet click after the tenth repetition.

I’ve started paying attention to signals of “fake progress”: twenty open tabs, endless note-taking, jumping between courses. When I notice that pattern, I try to do the opposite — close a few windows, choose one source, and stay with it until I can explain it in my own words.

The second piece is rest. Brains need time to integrate, but it’s hard to justify that when a new tutorial is always one click away. Learning slowly gives permission for walks, naps, and offline hours to count as part of the process instead of feeling like lost time.

I don’t always succeed at this. I still binge information and forget most of it. But having a name for the alternative — “learning slowly” — makes it easier to return to when I feel scattered. It’s not about being less ambitious. It’s about staying around long enough to actually enjoy what I’m learning.
